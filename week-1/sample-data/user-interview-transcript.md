# User Interview: Rachel Torres, Marketing Operations Manager
## GreenPath Studios — 45 employees, Series A

**Interviewer:** Sarah Chen (PM, TaskFlow)
**Date:** February 20, 2026
**Duration:** 32 minutes
**Context:** Rachel is a mid-market customer who has been using TaskFlow for 8 months. Her team of 12 uses it daily. She agreed to a research interview about how her team manages projects and where they hit friction.

---

**Sarah:** Thanks for taking the time, Rachel. I want to understand how your team actually works day to day -- what's going well, where things break down, and what an ideal workflow would look like. There are no wrong answers, and I'm not trying to sell you anything. Just learning.

**Rachel:** Sure, happy to help. I've got opinions.

**Sarah:** Great. Let's start with how you'd describe what your team does.

**Rachel:** We're the marketing ops team at GreenPath. We handle everything behind the scenes -- campaign execution, email workflows, event logistics, reporting. We're the people who make sure things actually get shipped, not just planned. There's 12 of us, and we run about 15-20 projects at any given time. Some are quick -- like a one-week email blast for a product launch. Others drag on for months -- like rebranding our entire event series.

**Sarah:** Walk me through a typical week for you.

**Rachel:** Monday morning I check what's due this week across all active projects. That takes me about 30 minutes because I have to click into each project individually to see the status. There's no single view that shows me "here's everything due this week across all projects." I end up keeping a parallel Google Sheet that I update manually. It's dumb but it's the only way I can see across everything.

Then I run our Monday standup. It's 15 minutes, and each person says what they're working on. I take notes in Google Docs and then go update TaskFlow after the meeting. That's another 20 minutes -- creating tasks, updating statuses, reassigning things. I'm basically a human sync engine between the meeting and the tool.

**Sarah:** Does anyone else on your team update TaskFlow directly?

**Rachel:** A few people do. Maybe 4 out of 12. The rest treat it as read-only. They check it to see what's assigned to them, but they don't update task status or add comments. So I end up doing it for them. I've tried to get everyone to use it more actively but the learning curve was steep at the beginning and now they've settled into habits.

**Sarah:** What happened during onboarding? What was that experience like?

**Rachel:** Honestly, it was rough. I signed up and went through the setup flow myself first. It took me about 25 minutes, and I'm technical-ish. Then I tried to get my team onboarded and it was chaos. The onboarding assumes everyone knows PM terminology -- "epics," "sprints," "story points." My team is marketers and designers. They don't think in sprints. They think in campaigns and deadlines.

I ended up making my own onboarding guide for my team. A Google Doc that says "here's how to see your tasks, here's how to mark something done, ignore everything else for now." That worked better than the official onboarding.

**Sarah:** How long until your team was productive with the tool?

**Rachel:** About 3 weeks before people stopped asking me basic questions every day. Two months before I felt like we were actually getting value from it. The problem is that the first month felt like we were doing more work, not less. Moving from Google Sheets to TaskFlow meant everyone had to learn a new system, and during that transition everything slowed down.

**Sarah:** You mentioned you keep a parallel Google Sheet. Tell me more about that.

**Rachel:** Yeah, it's embarrassing but it's true. I have a Google Sheet called "The Real Dashboard" and it has one row per project with columns for: project name, owner, status, next deadline, and blockers. I update it every Monday and Wednesday. The reason I need it is that TaskFlow doesn't have a cross-project view that shows me everything at the right level. The project list shows me projects but not what's due. The calendar shows me tasks but not which project they're in. There's no view that combines both.

I know other tools do this -- Monday.com has a "my work" view and Asana has portfolios. I've looked at switching, but the migration cost is too high at this point. We've got 8 months of data in TaskFlow.

**Sarah:** What would make you stop needing the Google Sheet?

**Rachel:** A dashboard that shows me: across all my projects, what's due this week, what's overdue, and where there's a blocker. Grouped by project. With the ability to click into any task and update it right there. Basically a portfolio-level view that's operational, not just a status report.

**Sarah:** If you could change three things about TaskFlow tomorrow, what would they be?

**Rachel:** Number one: that cross-project dashboard I just described. That would save me an hour a week minimum.

Number two: fix the notification system. I get 40+ emails a day from TaskFlow. I've tried turning them off but the settings are confusing and they don't seem to actually work. I turned off "task assignment" notifications and I still get emails when someone assigns me a task. At this point I've created a Gmail filter that archives everything from TaskFlow. Which means I miss important things too.

Number three: make onboarding simpler. I'm about to hire 2 more people and I'm dreading the onboarding process. If the official onboarding was "here's how to see your tasks and mark things done, you're done" instead of "here's every feature we have," my new hires could be productive in a day instead of a month.

**Sarah:** Let's talk about the notification issue more. What would ideal notifications look like for you?

**Rachel:** I want to be notified about exactly three things: when I'm assigned something new, when something I own is overdue, and when someone @mentions me in a comment. That's it. Everything else is noise. The current system tries to notify you about everything and then asks you to turn things off, which is backwards. It should start quiet and let me turn things on.

**Sarah:** You mentioned considering Monday.com and Asana. What keeps you on TaskFlow?

**Rachel:** Three things. First, the Slack integration is genuinely great. Being able to create tasks from Slack messages saves my team probably 2 hours a week. That's our killer feature. Second, the simplicity. When TaskFlow works, it's clean and fast. Monday.com and Asana feel bloated to me. Third, the migration cost. Moving 200+ projects and re-training 12 people is a project in itself. I'd need a really compelling reason.

**Sarah:** What would be a compelling enough reason to leave?

**Rachel:** If I still need my Google Sheet a year from now, I'll seriously evaluate switching. The whole point of buying a project management tool is to not maintain a parallel system. If I'm doing that, the tool isn't working.

**Sarah:** That's really helpful. Last question: is there anything I didn't ask about that you think we should know?

**Rachel:** One thing. Your AI features -- the task summary thing and the auto-generated descriptions. I've used them a few times and they're actually good. The summary feature is surprisingly helpful for my Monday reviews. But I wish I could tell it about our team's context. It generates generic descriptions, and I always have to edit them to match our terminology. If the AI knew that "GTM launch" means a specific process at our company, or that "Tier 1 campaign" means it needs VP approval, the output would be much more useful.

**Sarah:** Great feedback. Thank you, Rachel. This has been incredibly helpful.

**Rachel:** Happy to help. Seriously, fix the notifications. That one's driving me crazy.
